Governance Process Risk: The Governance Challenge No One Is Managing

By Ariel Goldfarb
Scroll

Boards always face the usual obstacles to effective decision making such as incomplete information, competing priorities, and the pressures of time. These are known challenges, and governance has developed effective tools to address them. But there is another factor, just as consequential to decision quality and present in every boardroom, that rarely receives the same level of attention. It is the conditions under which the table decides.

This is Governance Process Risk: the risk embedded not in what a board decides, but in how it decides. Whether the full range of judgment in the room actually shapes the outcome, or only the loudest voices do. Whether assumptions get examined or simply carried forward. Whether the alignment that forms reflects genuine shared understanding, or a room of nods covering different reads of what was just decided.

It is not a soft concept. Boards that reach decisions on unexamined ground produce outcomes that are more tenuous, more likely to be revisited, and more likely to fail under pressure. The cost shows up later, and rarely gets attributed to the process that produced it.

The shape of the risk is well documented. In Four Failures of Deliberating Groups, Cass Sunstein and Reid Hastie identify four predictable ways groups go wrong when they decide together.1

Four predictable ways groups go wrong
01 / Amplification

Errors get amplified rather than corrected,

as individual mistakes harden into group conviction through repetition.

02 / Cascade

Cascades take hold,

as the judgments of early speakers shape those of later ones, and successors withhold what they actually know.

03 / Polarization

Groups polarize,

ending up in more extreme positions than any single member arrived with.

04 / Shared-information bias

Shared information crowds out unshared:

what only one or two directors know rarely surfaces, because deliberation tends to recycle what everyone in the room already shares.

These are not exotic failure modes. Any director who has sat through a board meeting where the conversation felt foregone before it started, or where the strongest dissenting view got nodded past, has seen one of them in action.

The candor piece is the most visible. According to PwC's 2020 Annual Corporate Directors Survey, one in three directors finds it difficult to voice a dissenting view in the boardroom, and over half say it leads to muffled dissent.2

The most commonly cited reason is not disagreement with the decision itself but a desire to preserve relationships with peers. What people say in a board meeting and what they actually think are often not the same thing. The gap between the two is where decision quality gets lost.

But candor is only one of the four failures, and arguably not the most damaging. Cascades and shared-information bias do their work even when every voice in the room is candid. A board can be perfectly civil, perfectly transparent, and still arrive at a decision that no one in the room would have endorsed had the conversation gone differently.

This is the part conventional governance tools are not built for. Charters, agendas, executive sessions, board evaluations: these address composition and conduct. They do not address the deliberative process itself. The conditions that determine which assumptions get tested, which dissents get heard, and which alignments are real continue to operate underneath whatever the agenda says.

Process Risk operating underneath

That is what makes Process Risk distinctive.

It is the only governance risk most boards are not actively managing.

For the corporate legal community, this lands in a particular way.

Consider one executive's experience. She was hired as General Counsel at a financial services firm. Over time, she took on an additional role: Chief Operating Officer. When asked which title she values most, her answer is immediate: COO.

Not because she has abandoned her legal role, or because the work is not important. Because when she sat at the board table as General Counsel, the role defined what the board was prepared to receive from her. The COO title opened a different door: earlier conversations, broader questions, a conversation at the start of things rather than the tightening up afterward.

Same person.
Same skills.
Same table.
The only thing that changed was the title on her business card.

On its surface, the story is about a capable executive. Beneath that, it is itself an example of Process Risk in operation.

The board's process for receiving input was governed by title, not by the quality of the input. The cognitive contribution available to them did not change. What changed was whether the deliberative process let it in.

The pattern is reasonably common. According to the Thomson Reuters Institute's 2026 State of the Corporate Law Department Report, 86% of General Counsel believe their departments are significant contributors to organizational objectives, while only 17% of C-Suite executives agree. And 42% say the legal department contributes little or nothing at all.3

The gap is large enough that more than one reading is available: GCs may be overestimating their contribution, C-Suites may be undervaluing it, or both. What is not up for debate is the size of the gap. Either way, a substantial pool of analytical capacity is sitting outside the conversation it could most usefully be in.

Which is the opportunity. The GC role contains training that maps onto each of the four failures.

Identifying where a legal argument is more flimsy than it appears trains the same instinct that catches a flimsy board assumption. Separating fact from inference is the same work in a deposition or a strategy memo. And surfacing what is driving a stated view, rather than only what is being said, is the same discipline whether the speaker is a witness or a director.

None of this requires reinventing the role. The GC's existing analytical posture, already aimed at legal substance, simply turns toward the deliberative process itself. The corporate secretary handles the meeting; the GC, well positioned, handles the integrity of how the room is thinking.

For boards that want to take Process Risk seriously, this is among the most accessible places to start.

The resource is already in the room.
The training already exists.
The seat at the table is already there.
What is missing, in many cases, is the invitation.

The work that could most meaningfully elevate the contribution of the role, real and perceived, is the work most boards do not yet know to ask for.
